Advancing chemistry one step at a time.

Synergizing analytical instrumentation and multivariate statistics

Many of the world's untapped resources are too heterogeneous to see industrial use. Lignin is a prime example: it is a complex heteropolymer component of plant-based biomass that produces thousands of chemical moieties when deconstructed. Understanding these lignin breakdown product distributions requires a combination of analytical and mathematical tools. Within this domain, I specialize in the synergy between gas chromatography-mass spectroscopy and factor analysis.

Modeling the time evolution of chemical catalytic systems

Chemical kinetics is the study of time, and I want to see into the future. My work focuses on monitoring chemical processes over time and creating models that simulates their evolution. This can take the form of routine rate constant calculations, suggesting reaction pathways and networks, or building population balance equations from first principles.

Integrating concepts from biological and chemical engineering

Much of the world's most complex chemistry is conveniently packaged in the form of living organisms. We can use these microorganisms and their metabolic pathways to produce speciality commodities from chemical cocktails. Because traditional chemical engineering techniques cannot handle this alone, I design systems to produce such value-added products using chemical and biological means in a hybrid manner.

Competing in chess as a player and researcher.

Applying trends in human decision-making to online cheat detection

The integrity of any society relies on trust, and chess is no exception. Unfortunately, mechanisms behind fair play enforcement systems have been kept under lock and key for the better part of 30 years. This has seriously battered down trust and stunted research in the field. As a result, current anti-cheat mechanisms are simply not capable of catching the most elusive cheaters and are very prone to false positive detections. I'm intent on open collaboration in this area, and I champion player behavior analysis as the proper way to develop an online chess cheat detection framework.

Laarhoven, T.; Ponukumati, A.; Towards Transparent Cheat Detection in Online Chess: An Application of Human and Computer Decision-Making Preferences. Computers & Games. (2023). https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-031-34017-8_14.

Investing in promising small chemical firms.

Finding opportunities in the global small value stock universe

Small stocks, on average, have higher expected returns than large stocks. This is largely, but not entirely, due to small stocks being riskier than large stocks. Lack of coverage and unfamiliarity biases are partly to blame. Still, even small stocks with proper coverage and notoriety are illiquid and have capacity constraints, which make them generally unsuitable for institutional ownership. The risks of small stocks are more heterogeneous than those of large stocks, which begets a greater marginal benefit to index-like diverisification. More than that, I believe that investing in small companies, with promising but untapped ideas, has a much larger social footprint than investing in any other securities. Such firms tend to be in the energy and materials sectors, including many chemical firms.

Sharing analysis on stocks and ETFs with retail investors

Market efficiency is a godsend for (unskilled) retail investors, especially in the small cap universe. However, capital markets are only as efficient as market participants want them to be. It is important for all market participants to contribute to market efficiency, if only for their own self benefit. There is also tremendous societal value in teaching others about the power of diversification, asset pricing factors, and the basics of valuation theory. I meet these ends by writing various blogs and articles about stocks that interest me.
